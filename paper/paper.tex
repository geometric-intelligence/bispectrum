\begin{abstract}
Many machine-learning tasks are invariant under group actions: image
classification under planar rotation $\SO(2)$, signal classification
under translation, and spherical-image classification under 3D rotations
$\SO(3)$. The $G$-bispectrum is a principled \emph{complete} invariant
(it preserves all signal information up to the group action) but its
deployment has been hampered by quadratic cost and a patchwork of
group-specific implementations.
We present \texttt{bispectrum}, an open-source, fully unit-tested
PyTorch library that computes \emph{selective} $G$-bispectral
invariants for seven different group/domain pairs as drop-in
differentiable \texttt{nn.Module}s. Selectivity reduces the invariant
size from $O(|G|^2)$ for the full bispectrum to $O(|G|)$ while
remaining complete on generic signals.
On the theoretical side, we contribute a selective $\SO(3)$ invariant
on the sphere $S^2$ that achieves $\Theta(L^2)$ coefficients at
band-limit~$L$, matching the information-theoretic orbit-space lower
bound $(L{+}1)^2 - 3$ instead of the $O(L^3)$ of the full bispectrum.
We provide proof-of-concept evidence that the construction
retains enough information to recover signals up to rotation:
gradient-descent reconstruction from the selective coefficients
converges to rotated copies of the source signal across multiple
test signals (Section~\ref{sec:so3-recon}).
We benchmark the library on three datasets across three group/domain
pairs --- PCam under $C_8$, OrganMNIST3D under the octahedral group,
and Spherical~MNIST under $\SO(3)$ --- comparing against norm pooling,
gated pooling, Fourier-ELU pooling, max pooling, and (non-equivariant)
augmented CNN baselines. The library delivers near-exact rotation
invariance (test-set rotation-accuracy std $\sigma_{\mathrm{rot}} <
10^{-3}$) and a data-efficiency advantage over incomplete alternatives
in the low-data, moderate-capacity regime; the selective forward pass
runs in sub-millisecond time on GPU for finite groups.
Code is available at
\url{https://anonymous.4open.science/r/bispectrum/}.
\end{abstract}
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Many machine-learning tasks are invariant under the action of a symmetry
group $G$: histopathology classification under planar
rotations $C_n$, signal classification under translations,
volumetric organ classification under cube rotations $O$, and
spherical-image classification under 3D rotations $\SO(3)$. Equivariant
neural networks~\citep{cohen2016group, weiler2019general} enforce these
symmetries by construction in their feature maps, but at the point where
a prediction must be made any task that does not depend on the group
action --- classification, regression, detection --- still has to map
the equivariant features to an \emph{invariant} representation.

The \emph{$G$-bispectrum} is a complex-valued vector of triple-correlation
coefficients of the group Fourier transform. It has three properties that
matter for ML: (i)~\textbf{completeness} --- it preserves all signal
information up to the group action (the map is one-to-one on group
orbits); (ii)~\textbf{inversion} --- the signal can in principle be
recovered from the invariant up to its orbit; and
(iii)~\textbf{selectivity} --- a small $O(|G|)$ index subset of the
$O(|G|^2)$ full bispectrum still suffices for completeness on generic
signals~\citep{mataigne2024selective}.

Existing invariant pooling layers --- norm pooling, gate-based pooling,
max/sum aggregation~\citep{weiler2019general} --- are simple but
\emph{incomplete}: they collapse equivariant features to scalars and
discard information that distinguishes signals in different orbits.
The bispectrum offers a mathematically principled
alternative~\citep{giannakis1989bispectrum, kakarala2012bispectrum} and
has been used as a competitive pooling layer in equivariant
networks~\citep{sanborn2023bispectral, mataigne2024selective}.

\paragraph{Despite this, bispectral pooling has not been widely adopted,
for both theoretical and computational reasons.} Existing bispectral
constructions are scattered across theory papers, domain-specific
derivations, and small implementations, with no general-purpose PyTorch
library covering the discrete and continuous symmetry groups relevant
to modern geometric deep learning. Even for groups where
selectivity~\citep{mataigne2024selective} reduces the bispectrum from
$O(|G|^2)$ to $O(|G|)$ scalars (and the per-pass cost from $O(|G|^3)$
to $O(|G|^2)$), a user still needs a numerically stable
implementation, a consistent API, an inversion routine, tests, and
benchmarked runtime. On the theoretical side, the selective construction
of \citet{mataigne2024selective} applies to finite groups; the
continuous case of $\SO(3)$ acting on the sphere $S^2$ has remained open.

This paper presents \texttt{bispectrum}, a PyTorch~\citep{paszke2019pytorch}
library for computing bispectral invariants and, where available, their
inverses. The library supports the $C_n$, $D_n$, $\mathbb{T}^d$, disk,
$S^2$, and octahedral domains as differentiable \texttt{nn.Module}s
intended to plug into standard ML workflows. Beyond the software contribution,
the paper makes one technical contribution that arose during
implementation: a selective $\SO(3)$ bispectrum on $S^2$ with
$\Theta(L^2)$ coefficients (Section~\ref{sec:so3-selective}).

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Software contribution.} A PyTorch library implementing
    selective $G$-bispectra for \emph{seven different group/domain
    pairs} (a \emph{domain} is the underlying space on which the group
    acts: $C_n$ on $C_n$, $\SO(2)$ on $S^1$, $\mathbb{T}^d$, $D_n$,
    $\SO(2)$ on the disk, $\SO(3)$ on $S^2$, and the octahedral group
    $O$) as drop-in differentiable \texttt{nn.Module}s with a consistent
    API, GPU-optimized forward passes ($<$1\,ms for finite groups),
    precomputed CG buffers, and a fully unit-tested codebase
    (96.8\% line coverage).
  \item \textbf{Selective $\SO(3)$ bispectrum on $S^2$.} We construct
    an invariant on $S^2$ that uses $\Theta(L^2)$ scalar coefficients
    (matching the orbit-space dimension $(L{+}1)^2 - 3$ up to a
    constant factor) instead of the $O(L^3)$ of the full bispectrum,
    extending the BFS-style finite-group construction
    of~\citet{mataigne2024selective} to the continuous case
    $\SO(3)$ on $S^2$ (Section~\ref{sec:so3-selective}). We
    demonstrate empirically that the construction retains enough
    information to recover band-limited signals up to rotation:
    gradient-descent reconstruction from $\beta_{\mathrm{sel}}$
    converges to rotated copies of the source signal across all
    test cases (Figure~\ref{fig:so3-reconstruction}). A formal proof
    of completeness for the selective construction is left to future
    work.
  \item \textbf{Empirical validation.} A benchmark of $G$-bispectral
    pooling across three ML tasks --- PCam under $C_8$, OrganMNIST3D
    under the octahedral group $O$, and Spherical~MNIST under $\SO(3)$
    --- against norm pooling, gated pooling, Fourier-ELU pooling, max
    pooling, and (non-equivariant) augmented-CNN baselines. Complete
    bispectral invariants provide a data-efficiency advantage in the
    low-data, moderate-capacity regime while converging to incomplete
    alternatives at high capacity.
\end{enumerate}
Together, these contributions provide the first general-purpose,
drop-in differentiable library for $G$-bispectral invariants and the
first practical extension of the selective construction from finite
groups to $\SO(3)$ on $S^2$.

\section{Mathematical Background}
\label{sec:background}

\paragraph{Notation.}
A \emph{group} $G$ is a set with an associative product, identity, and inverse
(e.g.\ rotations of the plane, translations of an image grid). $G$ is
\emph{abelian} if elements commute (e.g.\ cyclic groups $C_n$, the torus
$\mathbb{T}^d$) and \emph{non-abelian} otherwise (e.g.\ $\SO(3)$, the
dihedral groups $D_n$, the octahedral group $O$). A
\emph{representation} $\rho$ assigns to each $g \in G$ a unitary matrix
$\rho(g)$ such that $\rho(gh) = \rho(g)\rho(h)$; an \emph{irreducible
representation} (\emph{irrep}) admits no nontrivial invariant subspace.
The \emph{dual} $\hat{G}$ denotes the set of equivalence classes of
irreps; for finite $G$, $|\hat{G}| = O(|G|)$. A \emph{group action} of
$G$ on a domain $X$ is a map $G \times X \to X$, $(g, x) \mapsto g \cdot x$,
compatible with the group product; it induces an action on functions
$f : X \to \R$ by $(f \circ g)(x) := f(g \cdot x)$. Two signals are in
the same \emph{orbit} if one is the image of the other under some
$g \in G$; an \emph{invariant} of $f$ is a function constant on each
orbit. For compact $G$ there is a unique normalised translation-invariant
measure $d\mu$ called the \emph{Haar measure}; finite $G$ is the special
case where $d\mu$ is the counting measure. ``\emph{Lebesgue-almost-every
$f$}'' means ``every $f$ outside a measure-zero algebraic subvariety of
the signal space.'' The groups and domains used in this paper are: the
cyclic group $C_n$ (discrete planar rotations), the dihedral group $D_n$
(rotations and reflections of a regular $n$-gon), the torus
$\mathbb{T}^d = (S^1)^d$ (multi-channel periodic signals), $\SO(2)$
acting on the disk (continuous planar rotations of a 2D image), $\SO(3)$
acting on the sphere $S^2$ (continuous 3D rotations of a spherical
signal), and the chiral octahedral group $O$ ($24$ proper rotations of
the cube, acting on $3$D voxel grids).

\subsection{Equivariant features and invariant maps}

A neural network layer $\Phi$ is \emph{$G$-equivariant} if its output
transforms predictably under the group action:
$[\pi_{\mathrm{out}}(g) \circ \Phi](f) = \Phi(\pi_{\mathrm{in}}(g) f)$
for all $g \in G$~\citep{cohen2016group, weiler2019general}. Here $f$ is
an input signal (a function on the domain $X$),
$\pi_{\mathrm{in}}, \pi_{\mathrm{out}}$ are the (representations of the)
$G$-actions on the input and output feature spaces of $\Phi$, and the
composition $\pi_{\mathrm{out}}(g) \circ \Phi$ is the action of $g$ on
the output side. Stacking equivariant layers preserves equivariance, but
any task that requires a prediction independent of the group
action---classification, regression, detection---must eventually map
equivariant features to \emph{invariant} ones (constant on each orbit).

Table~\ref{tab:nonlinearity-comparison} summarises the trade-offs between

pooling layers. Norm pooling and gated nonlinearities are exact
invariants but \emph{incomplete}: they discard the phase of Fourier

nonlinearities preserve completeness but sacrifice exactness through
aliasing~\citep{weiler2019general}. The full bispectrum is exact and
complete but costs $O(|G|^2)$ coefficients. The \emph{selective}
bispectrum (introduced below) preserves both exactness and completeness
while using only $O(|G|)$ coefficients on generic signals.

\subsection{The $G$-bispectrum and selective bispectra}

Let $G$ be a compact group with dual $\hat{G}$ and irreps
$\{\rho_i\}_{i \in \hat{G}}$ of dimensions $d_i$. The \emph{group Fourier
transform}~\citep{diaconis1990fourier} of a signal $f : G \to \C$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fourier}
  \hat{f}(\rho_i)
  = \int_G f(g)\, \rho_i(g)\, d\mu(g)
  \quad\in\C^{d_i \times d_i},
\end{equation}
which specialises to $\sum_{g \in G} f(g)\, \rho_i(g)$ when $G$ is finite
(counting measure). The Clebsch--Gordan (CG) decomposition
$\rho_i \otimes \rho_j \cong \bigoplus_{k \in \hat{G}} m_{ij}^k \rho_k$
expresses the tensor product of two irreps as a direct sum of irreps
(with integer multiplicities $m_{ij}^k \ge 0$); the change-of-basis is
realised by the \emph{CG isometry}
$C_{ij}^k : V_{\rho_k} \to V_{\rho_i} \otimes V_{\rho_j}$, projecting the
tensor product onto its $\rho_k$-isotypic component. For each admissible
triple $(i, j, k)$ with $m_{ij}^k > 0$, the \emph{bispectrum} is the
scalar
\begin{equation}
  \label{eq:bispectrum-def}
  \beta_{i,j}^{k}(f)
  := \bigl\langle\, (C_{ij}^k)^{\dagger}\,
        \bigl(\hat{f}(\rho_i) \otimes \hat{f}(\rho_j)\bigr),\,
        \hat{f}(\rho_k) \bigr\rangle.
\end{equation}
Each $\beta_{i,j}^{k}$ is invariant under the action $f \mapsto f \circ g$
and, taken over all admissible triples, the bispectrum
$\{\beta_{i,j}^{k}\}_{(i,j,k)}$ is a \emph{complete} invariant: it
separates orbits for generic signals~\citep{kakarala2012bispectrum}. For
finite $G$, $|\hat G| = O(|G|)$, so the full bispectrum has $O(|G|^2)$
scalar coefficients (and is naturally indexed by $(i,j) \in \hat{G}^2$
with $k$ ranging over the CG-allowed irreps).
Equation~\eqref{eq:bispectrum-def} is stated for $G$ acting on itself;
for homogeneous spaces of the form $G/H$ (e.g.\ $\SO(3)$ on $S^2$, or
$\SO(2)$ on the disk), the same construction applies after expanding $f$
in a basis adapted to $G/H$ (spherical harmonics for $S^2$, Bessel modes
on the disk). The explicit specialisation for $S^2$ is in
Definition~\ref{def:app-bispectrum}.

\paragraph{Scalar vs.\ matrix (vector) bispectrum.}
Throughout this paper the term \emph{scalar bispectrum} refers to the
single complex number $\beta_{i,j}^{k}(f)$ obtained from the inner
product in Eq.~\eqref{eq:bispectrum-def}---i.e., the CG-contracted
scalar associated with each admissible triple. In the literature one
also encounters the \emph{matrix} or \emph{vector} bispectrum, which
keeps the un-contracted matrix
$(C_{ij}^k)^\dagger (\hat f(\rho_i) \otimes \hat f(\rho_j))$ before
pairing it with $\hat f(\rho_k)$. The two are related by a single
trace/inner-product step; the scalar form is what the library outputs
and what we analyse for $\SO(3)$ on $S^2$.

\emph{Selective bispectra.} \citet{mataigne2024selective} show that for
finite groups, a breadth-first search (BFS) over the Kronecker product
table of $\hat{G}$ selects $O(|G|)$ bispectral coefficients
$\beta_{i,j}^{k}$ that remain complete; \citet{myers2025selective}
extend this to the disk. This
reduction from quadratic to linear is the key to practical deployment:
for example, the bispectrum of the octahedral group drops from $576$ to
$172$ coefficients.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Equivariant network libraries.}
\texttt{escnn}~\citep{cesa2022program} and \texttt{e3nn}~\citep{geiger2022e3nn}
provide general-purpose equivariant convolution layers for 2D and 3D
groups respectively, and \texttt{lie-nn}~\citep{batatia2023general}
provides general Lie-group representation primitives (irreducible
representations, Clebsch--Gordan decompositions, tensor products) on
top of which custom equivariant layers can be built.  These libraries
implement equivariant \emph{feature maps} but leave the choice of
invariant pooling to the user (typically norm or gate). Our library is
complementary: it provides the invariant map as a drop-in
\texttt{nn.Module} that plugs into architectures built with any of these
frameworks.

\paragraph{Bispectral approaches in neural networks.}
\citet{sanborn2023bispectral} introduced bispectral neural networks (BNNs),
using the full $G$-bispectrum as an invariant nonlinearity for $C_n$ and $D_n$.
\citet{oreiller2022bispectral} applied bispectral CNNs to medical imaging
with a fixed set of hand-selected triples. Both use the full (quadratic-cost)
bispectrum and provide implementations tied to specific architectures.
Our library generalizes to seven group/domain pairs, defaults to the
$O(|G|)$ selective reduction~\citep{mataigne2024selective}, and exposes
a framework-agnostic API.
\citet{chevalley2024bispectral} explore bispectral signatures for data
characterisation, using bispectra as descriptors rather than learnable
pooling primitives.
Outside the equivariant-network setting,
\texttt{PyBispectra}~\citep{binns2025pybispectra} computes bispectrum
and bicoherence statistics of one-dimensional time-series signals for
electrophysiology applications such as phase-amplitude coupling and
time-delay estimation; the underlying 1D bispectrum is translation-invariant
by construction, but the toolbox is not designed as a neural-network
pooling layer.

\paragraph{Bispectra on homogeneous and non-homogeneous spaces.}
The selective $G$-bispectrum
construction~\citep{mataigne2024selective} reduces the cost of the
$G$-bispectrum from $O(|G|^2)$ to $O(|G|)$ scalars on finite groups.
\citet{myers2025selective} extend the selective construction to the
disk, where $\SO(2)$ acts on a non-homogeneous space.
\citet{kakarala2012bispectrum} treats the $\SO(3)$-on-$\SO(3)$
bispectrum and the closely related sphere case via Wigner-$D$ irreps.
The matrix (vector) bispectrum on $S^2$ is the homogeneous-space form
on which our scalar contractions and CG power augmentation are built.

\paragraph{CG product nonlinearities and tensor products.}
Tensor field networks~\citep{thomas2018tensor} and
MACE~\citep{batatia2022mace} use iterated Clebsch--Gordan products
to build equivariant interactions. These products are exact and complete
in principle, but each layer incurs $O(L^3)$ cost per CG contraction.
\citet{xie2025price} analyse the \emph{price of freedom} in tensor
product architectures, showing that maintaining full expressivity through
CG products becomes expensive at high angular momenta.
The selective bispectrum is empirically complete (proof of concept
in Figure~\ref{fig:so3-reconstruction}; finite-group case proved
in~\citet{mataigne2024selective}) in a single $O(L^2)$ pass for
$\SO(3)$ or $O(|G|)$ pass for finite groups, avoiding iterated
contractions.

\paragraph{Invariant theory and scattering.}
The wavelet scattering transform~\citep{mallat2012scattering} provides
translation-invariant representations via iterated modulus operations;
\citet{esteves2018spherical} extend this to the sphere. Unlike the
$G$-bispectrum, scattering representations are stable but generally
incomplete---they discard inter-scale phase information.
The $G$-bispectrum's completeness
guarantee~\citep{giannakis1989bispectrum, kakarala2012bispectrum} is
its distinguishing theoretical advantage.

\section{The \texttt{bispectrum} Library}
\label{sec:library}

\subsection{Design and implementation}
\label{sec:design}

Every group/domain pair is exposed as a \texttt{torch.nn.Module}
named \texttt{\{Group\}on\{Domain\}} (e.g.\ \texttt{SO3onS2}).
Each module exposes a uniform API: \texttt{forward(f)} returns the
selective bispectral invariants of $f$; \texttt{fourier(f)} returns the
underlying group Fourier coefficients; and, where available,
\texttt{invert(beta)} returns the reconstructed signal up to
group-action indeterminacy.
Modules accept raw real-valued inputs, handle the Fourier transform
internally, and default to $O(|G|)$ selective coefficients
(\texttt{selective=False} gives the full $O(|G|^2)$ set).
Clebsch--Gordan (CG) matrices, discrete Fourier transform (DFT) kernels,
and Bessel roots (used for the radial discretisation of the disk
in \texttt{SO2onDisk}) are precomputed at construction and stored as
non-learnable buffers; this lets \texttt{.to(device)} migrate the
module transparently and ensures that forward passes incur zero
recomputation.
The only dependencies are PyTorch~\citep{paszke2019pytorch},
NumPy~\citep{harris2020numpy}, and
\texttt{torch\_harmonics}~\citep{bonev2023spherical}
(for \texttt{SO3onS2}).
For $\SO(3)$ on $S^2$, reduced CG matrices are built in parallel and
cached to disk; all forward methods are batch-native, using
\texttt{torch.einsum}/\texttt{torch.bmm} for non-abelian modules and
\texttt{torch.fft} for abelian ones.
The test suite covers 96.8\% of lines across all modules.
A minimal usage example is in Appendix~\ref{app:api-example}.

$\ell_{\max}{=}16$ takes 26.5\,ms, dominated by the spherical harmonic
transform (via \texttt{torch\_harmonics}~\citep{bonev2023spherical}).
Selective coefficient counts confirm the $O(|G|)$ scaling:
$C_{128}$ uses 128 coefficients vs.\ 8{,}256 full (64$\times$
reduction); $\mathbb{T}^2$ at $|G|{=}1{,}024$ uses 1{,}024 vs.\
524{,}800 full (512$\times$). Inversion is fast for abelian groups
(9--68\,ms). GPU throughput scales linearly with batch size,
exceeding $10^7$~samples/s for $C_n$ and $\mathbb{T}^2$
(Appendix~\ref{app:bench}).

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

groups: 2D histopathology classification under $C_8$ symmetry (PCam,
Section~\ref{sec:pcam}), 3D organ classification under octahedral symmetry
(OrganMNIST3D, Section~\ref{sec:organ3d}), and spherical digit
classification under full $\SO(3)$ (Spherical MNIST,
Section~\ref{sec:smnist}).

\subsection{Spherical MNIST: SO(3) invariance on the sphere}
\label{sec:smnist}

The preceding experiments validate bispectral pooling for finite groups
($C_8$ and the octahedral group $O$). To evaluate the library's continuous
group support, we test \texttt{SO3onS2}---the $\SO(3)$ bispectrum on the
2-sphere---on Spherical MNIST~\citep{cohen2018spherical}, the standard
benchmark for rotation invariance on $S^2$.

\paragraph{Setup.}
MNIST digits are projected onto the sphere via stereographic projection and
sampled on a $64 \times 128$ equiangular grid. We compute the selective
bispectrum at $\ell_{\max} = 15$, producing 384 complex invariant
coefficients. To form the network input we apply a sign-preserving
$\operatorname{sign}(x)\log(1+|x|)$ transform separately to the real
and imaginary parts of each coefficient, yielding 768 real features.
By Lemma~\ref{lem:app-parity}, exactly one of the two log1p features
per coefficient is structurally zero on real signals (real for even
parity, imaginary for odd parity); we keep both channels because the
transform is sign-preserving and per-channel injective. The features
are fed to a 3-layer MLP (232K parameters total). Three baselines
provide controls:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Power Spectrum + MLP} (11K params): computes only the
    rotation-invariant power spectrum $\|f_\ell\|^2$ per degree $\ell$,
    discarding all phase coupling between harmonics. Invariant but
    \emph{incomplete}.
  \item \textbf{Power Spectrum + MLP (matched)} (166K params): same
    incomplete invariant features but with a larger MLP (166K),
    isolating the effect of feature completeness from model capacity.
  \item \textbf{Standard CNN} (185K params): a conventional CNN operating
    directly on the equirectangular image. Not rotation-invariant.
\end{itemize}
Following \citet{cohen2018spherical}, we evaluate under three
protocols: train on non-rotated / test on non-rotated (NR/NR), train on
rotated / test on rotated (R/R), and the most challenging train on
non-rotated / test on rotated (NR/R). Each configuration is run with three
seeds. Rotation robustness is assessed by applying 10 random $\SO(3)$
rotations to the entire test set and measuring accuracy variance.

\section{Selective $\SO(3)$ bispectrum on $S^2$}
\label{sec:so3-selective}

\subsection{Setup}

A real signal $f : S^2 \to \R$ band-limited at degree~$L$ expands in
spherical harmonics as
$f(\theta,\varphi) = \sum_{\ell=0}^{L}\sum_{m=-\ell}^{\ell}
a_\ell^m \SH_\ell^m(\theta,\varphi)$ with the reality constraint
$a_\ell^{-m} = (-1)^m \conj{a_\ell^m}$, so it is parameterised by
$(L{+}1)^2$ real scalars
(Definition~\ref{def:app-bispectrum},
eq.~\eqref{eq:app-sh-expansion}). On $S^2 = \SO(3)/\SO(2)_z$, the
relevant irreducible representations of $\SO(3)$ are
$\{\rho_\ell\}_{\ell\ge 0}$ with $V_{\rho_\ell}$ the
$(2\ell{+}1)$-dimensional space of degree-$\ell$ spherical harmonics
and Wigner-$D$ action $\rho_\ell(g) = D^\ell(g)$. In the irrep notation
of~\citet{mataigne2024selective}, the Fourier coefficient at
$\rho_\ell$ is
$\mathcal{F}(f)_{\rho_\ell}=\mathbf{F}_\ell\in\C^{2\ell+1}$; we write
$\mathbf{F}_\ell$ throughout. The scalar
$\rho_\ell$-isotypic component of the matrix-valued bispectrum
$\beta_{\rho_{\ell_1},\rho_{\ell_2}}(f)$ is the familiar
\begin{equation}\label{eq:s6-bispectrum}
  \beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}
  = \!\!\!\sum_{\substack{m_1,m_2,m\\ m_1+m_2=m}}\!\!\!
    \CG{\ell_1}{m_1}{\ell_2}{m_2}{\ell}{m}\,
    a_{\ell_1}^{m_1}\,a_{\ell_2}^{m_2}\,\conj{a_\ell^m},
\end{equation}
the specialisation of eq.~\eqref{eq:bispectrum-def} to $\SO(3)$-on-$S^2$.
The full bispectrum has $\Theta(L^3)$ such scalars; the orbit space
$\R^{(L+1)^2}/\SO(3)$ has dimension $(L{+}1)^2 - 3$, so any complete
invariant requires $\Omega(L^2)$ independent components
(Proposition~\ref{prop:app-lower-bound}). The Mataigne chain
$\beta_{1,\ell,\ell+1}$ for $\ell=0,\dots,L{-}1$ provides only
$\sim 3L$ scalars and cannot achieve this bound.

\subsection{Selective construction and algorithm}

Let $L_{\mathrm{seed}} := \min(L, 4)$. The \emph{seed index set}
collects all admissible bispectral triples up to $L_{\mathrm{seed}}$,
\[
  \mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{seed}} :=
    \{(\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell) :
      0\le \ell_1\le \ell_2 \le L_{\mathrm{seed}},\,
      \abs{\ell_1{-}\ell_2}\le \ell
        \le \min(\ell_1{+}\ell_2, L_{\mathrm{seed}})\},
\]
of constant size $\le 50$ once $L\ge 4$, matching the implementation
seed (Algorithm~\ref{alg:so3-augmented}). For each high-degree
target $\ell\ge 5$, we use a size-$(2\ell{+}1)$ \emph{bootstrap
block} $\mathcal{T}_\ell$, which for $\ell\ge 8$ is the closed-form
family of eq.~\eqref{eq:app-Tell}
(Definition~\ref{def:app-selective}) and for $5\le\ell\le 7$ is the
explicit small block $\mathcal{T}_\ell^{\mathrm{small}}$ tabulated
in Algorithm~\ref{alg:so3-augmented}. Both versions consist of
entries linear in $\mathbf{F}_\ell$ with coefficients depending only
on already-recovered lower degrees. The \emph{selective $\SO(3)$
bispectrum on $S^2$} is then the concatenation
\begin{equation}\label{eq:s6-betasel}
  \beta_{\mathrm{sel}}(f) := \bigl\{\beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}\bigr\}_{
    (\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell)\in
      \mathcal{S}_\beta},\qquad
  \mathcal{S}_\beta := \mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{seed}}\,\cup\,
    \bigcup_{\ell=5}^{L} \mathcal{T}_\ell.
\end{equation}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:app-parity}, each scalar entry contributes
exactly one real degree of freedom on the real-signal slice (real
for even parity, purely imaginary for odd parity); we record that
single real number per entry.
Algorithm~\ref{alg:selective-bsp-so3} writes this as a Mataigne-style
BFS over selected triples; the implementation-grade index set
(Algorithm~\ref{alg:so3-augmented} in Appendix~\ref{app:so3-proof})
augments $\mathcal{S}_\beta$ with mandatory even self-couplings and
optional CG-power scalars for numerical robustness, and is a strict
superset of $\beta_{\mathrm{sel}}$.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Selective $\SO(3)$ bispectrum on $S^2$
  (BFS form, mirroring~\citet{mataigne2024selective}, Alg.~1).}
\label{alg:selective-bsp-so3}
\small
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} band-limit $L$,
  seed cutoff $L_{\mathrm{seed}} = \min(L,4)$
\STATE $\mathcal{L}_\beta \leftarrow$ all admissible triples
  $(\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell)$ with $\ell\le L_{\mathrm{seed}}$
  \COMMENT{seed block $\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{seed}}$}
\FOR{$\ell = L_{\mathrm{seed}}{+}1, \dotsc, L$}
  \STATE append the bootstrap block $\mathcal{T}_\ell$ to $\mathcal{L}_\beta$
    \COMMENT{$2\ell{+}1$ entries linear in $\mathbf{F}_\ell$;
      $\mathcal{T}_\ell^{\mathrm{small}}$ for $\ell\le 7$,
      eq.~\eqref{eq:app-Tell} for $\ell\ge 8$}
\ENDFOR
\RETURN $\beta_{\mathrm{sel}}(f) = \{\beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}(f) :
  (\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell)\in\mathcal{L}_\beta\}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\noindent
The output size is
$|\beta_{\mathrm{sel}}|
 = |\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{seed}}| + \sum_{\ell=5}^{L}(2\ell{+}1)
 = \Theta(L^2)$,
matching the orbit-space lower bound up to a constant
(Proposition~\ref{prop:app-output-size}).

\begin{remark}[CG power augmentation in the implementation]
The library appends the CG power spectrum
$P_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell} := \lVert(\mathbf{F}_{\ell_1}\otimes
\mathbf{F}_{\ell_2})|_\ell\rVert^2$
(Definition~\ref{def:app-cg-power}) at $O(L^2)$ extra cost. The pure
linear bootstrap submatrix $A_\ell\in\R^{(2\ell+1)\times(2\ell+1)}$
assembled from $\mathcal{T}_\ell$ alone is rank-deficient on the
real-signal slice (see
Remark~\ref{rem:app-Tell-rank-deficit}); appending the CG power
scalars (and the mandatory even self-couplings of
Algorithm~\ref{alg:so3-augmented}) restores per-degree numerical
robustness, and is what the implementation does unconditionally.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Motivation.}
Three structural facts justify this construction. (i)~The output
size $|\beta_{\mathrm{sel}}| = \Theta(L^2)$ matches the orbit-space
dimension $(L{+}1)^2 - 3$ up to a constant factor
(Proposition~\ref{prop:app-lower-bound}); no $G$-invariant of
strictly lower complexity can be complete. (ii)~The BFS-style
construction directly mirrors the selective bispectrum of
\citet{mataigne2024selective} for finite groups, where the same
$2\ell{+}1$ entries-per-shell pattern is provably complete; we
extend their template from finite $G$ to the continuous case
$\SO(3)$ on $S^2$. (iii)~The matrix bispectrum on $L^1(\SO(3))$ is
known to be injective on dense
orbits~\citep{kakarala1992triple,kakarala2012bispectrum},
suggesting the scalar CG contraction $\beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}$
inherits enough information to separate orbits in the band-limited
setting, at least generically. We do not give a formal proof of
completeness here; instead we provide direct empirical evidence
below, and leave a closed-form proof for future work.

\subsection{Empirical proof of concept: bispectrum reconstruction}
\label{sec:so3-recon}

To test whether $\beta_{\mathrm{sel}}$ retains enough information to
recover signals up to rotation, we run a direct inversion experiment.
We take two band-limited signals $f:S^2\to\R$ at $L=15$ (one from
each of the first two Spherical~MNIST classes), compute
$\beta_{\mathrm{sel}}(f)$, and reconstruct $f$ by gradient descent on
the spherical-harmonic coefficients $\hat a_\ell^m$, minimising
$\lVert \beta_{\mathrm{sel}}(\hat f) - \beta_{\mathrm{sel}}(f)
\rVert^2$ from a random initialisation. After convergence, $\hat f$
is aligned to $f$ by the optimal $\hat R\in\SO(3)$ (degree-1
alignment plus Wigner-$D$ refinement).

    \emph{Recon $\hat R \cdot \hat f$} shows the gradient-descent

\paragraph{Takeaways.}
On both signals the bispectrum residual reaches
$\lVert\Delta\beta\rVert/\lVert\beta\rVert\sim 10^{-3}$ at
convergence, and the aligned image residual
$\lVert \hat R\cdot\hat f - f\rVert / \lVert f\rVert$ is $0.23$
(class~$0$) and $0.11$ (class~$1$); the recovered $\hat f$ visually
matches $f$ up to rotation in both cases
(Figure~\ref{fig:so3-reconstruction}). Residual image error is
dominated by gradient-descent local optima rather than by missing
invariant information: each reconstruction is initialised from a
single random point and run with no restarts. We read this as
proof-of-concept evidence that the selective coefficients carry
enough information to recover band-limited signals up to $\SO(3)$; a
formal proof of completeness for $\beta_{\mathrm{sel}}$ remains an
open question.


\section{Discussion and Conclusion}
\label{sec:discussion}

We have presented \texttt{bispectrum}, a PyTorch library that makes selective
$G$-bispectral invariants practical for machine learning. The library covers
seven group/domain pairs with a uniform \texttt{nn.Module} interface and
required one technical contribution during implementation: a
selective $\SO(3)$ bispectrum on $S^2$ achieving the optimal
$\Theta(L^2)$ coefficient count
(Section~\ref{sec:so3-selective}). Table~\ref{tab:benchmarks} confirms that the
selective forward pass adds negligible overhead to training pipelines
($<$1\,ms for all finite-group modules on a single GPU). Across three
experimental settings, bispectral pooling provides numerically exact
rotation invariance (note that other pooling strategies, such as norm
or max pooling, also yield exact invariance; the bispectrum's
distinguishing property is its richer, completeness-oriented invariant,
not invariance per se) and a data-efficiency advantage over incomplete
alternatives in the low-data / moderate-capacity regime, where the
$\Theta(L^2)$ invariant map provides a stronger inductive bias that
partially compensates for limited training data.

\paragraph{When not to use the bispectrum.}
The data-efficiency advantage of complete invariants is most pronounced
in the low-data, moderate-capacity regime.
At high model capacity (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}), incomplete
alternatives such as max pooling can match or surpass bispectral pooling,
likely because the network learns to compensate for missing invariant
information.
Each bispectrum module is also invariant to a \emph{specific} group
(e.g.\ rotations only, or translations only); for tasks that need
invariance to multiple or unknown symmetries, learned invariance via
data augmentation or group-equivariant networks with flexible symmetry
may be a better fit.
Completeness is a property of the \emph{map}, not of downstream task
performance: a complete invariant provides a stronger inductive bias,
but does not guarantee superior accuracy on every task.
Invertibility of the bispectrum for generic signals was first proved by
\citet{giannakis1989bispectrum}; \citet{kakarala2012bispectrum}
extended this to compact groups.
For $\SO(3)$ on $S^2$, computational cost grows as $O(L^2)$ per forward
pass; at very large band-limits this may become a bottleneck relative
to simpler pooling strategies.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Inversion for $\SO(3)$ on $S^2$ is not yet shipped as a library API;
only the selective forward map is. The reconstruction experiment of
Figure~\ref{fig:so3-reconstruction} supports completeness of
$\beta_{\mathrm{sel}}$ as a working hypothesis, but a closed-form
proof of completeness for the selective construction is left to
future work; in particular, a full characterisation of the
degenerate set and a numerical-conditioning analysis at large
band-limits are open. Our experimental comparisons do not include
tensor-product architectures (e3nn, MACE) or wavelet scattering,
which target different input assumptions and would require
non-trivial adaptation to the benchmarks used here.

\paragraph{Future work.}
Key directions include: implementing $\SO(3)$ inversion from selective
bispectral coefficients as a first-class library API; a closed-form
completeness proof for $\beta_{\mathrm{sel}}$;
numerical conditioning analysis at large band-limits;
broader framework integration (the library is intentionally
framework-agnostic and already drops in as an invariant module for
architectures built on \texttt{escnn}~\citep{cesa2022program} or
\texttt{e3nn}~\citep{geiger2022e3nn});
and applications to cryo-EM reconstruction and molecular property
prediction.

\subsection{Setup and notation}
\label{sec:app-setup}

\begin{definition}[Band-limited signal on $S^2$]
A signal $f : S^2 \to \R$ is \emph{band-limited} at degree $L$ if its
spherical harmonic expansion
\begin{equation}\label{eq:app-sh-expansion}
  f(\theta, \varphi)
  = \sum_{\ell=0}^{L} \sum_{m=-\ell}^{\ell}
    a_\ell^m \, \SH_\ell^m(\theta, \varphi)
\end{equation}
has $a_\ell^m = 0$ for all $\ell > L$. We write
$\mathbf{F}_\ell = (a_\ell^{-\ell}, \dotsc, a_\ell^{\ell})
  \in \C^{2\ell+1}$
for the coefficient vector at degree~$\ell$.
\end{definition}

\noindent
For real $f$, the conjugation symmetry
$a_\ell^{-m} = (-1)^m \conj{a_\ell^m}$ holds, so
$\mathbf{F}_\ell$ has $2\ell+1$ real degrees of freedom.
The total signal dimension is
$\dim = \sum_{\ell=0}^{L}(2\ell+1) = (L+1)^2$.

\begin{definition}[Rotation action]
Under $g \in \SO(3)$, the spherical harmonic coefficients transform as
$\mathbf{F}_\ell \mapsto D^\ell(g)\,\mathbf{F}_\ell$,
where $D^\ell$ is the $(2\ell+1)$-dimensional Wigner~$D$-matrix.
\end{definition}

\noindent
In the irrep notation of~\citet{mataigne2024selective},
$\{\rho_\ell\}_{\ell\ge 0}$ are the (unitary) irreducible
representations of $\SO(3)$ with $V_{\rho_\ell}$ the
$(2\ell{+}1)$-dimensional space of degree-$\ell$ spherical harmonics
and $\rho_\ell(g)=D^\ell(g)$; the Fourier coefficient of $f$ at
$\rho_\ell$ is $\mathcal{F}(f)_{\rho_\ell}=\mathbf{F}_\ell$. We use
$\mathbf{F}_\ell$ as the working symbol throughout.

\begin{definition}[Bispectrum on $S^2$]\label{def:app-bispectrum}
For each triple $(\ell_1, \ell_2, \ell)$ satisfying the triangle
inequality $\abs{\ell_1 - \ell_2} \le \ell \le \ell_1 + \ell_2$,
the \emph{bispectral coefficient} is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:app-bispectrum}
  \beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}
  = \sum_{\substack{m_1,\,m_2,\,m \\ m_1+m_2=m}}
    \CG{\ell_1}{m_1}{\ell_2}{m_2}{\ell}{m}\;
    a_{\ell_1}^{m_1}\,a_{\ell_2}^{m_2}\,\conj{a_\ell^m},
\end{equation}
where $\CG{\ell_1}{m_1}{\ell_2}{m_2}{\ell}{m}$ denotes the
Clebsch--Gordan coefficient
$\langle \ell_1, m_1;\, \ell_2, m_2 \mid \ell, m \rangle$.
Each $\beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}$ is a scalar; for real~$f$ it is
real when $\ell_1{+}\ell_2{+}\ell$ is even and purely imaginary when
$\ell_1{+}\ell_2{+}\ell$ is odd (Lemma~\ref{lem:app-parity}).
\end{definition}

Definition~\ref{def:app-bispectrum} is the specialization of the
general $G$-bispectrum (eq.~\eqref{eq:bispectrum-def}) to the case
$G = \SO(3)$ acting on~$S^2$, where the irreps are Wigner-$D$
matrices and the Fourier coefficients are spherical harmonic
coefficients~$a_\ell^m$.

\begin{definition}[CG power spectrum, optional diagnostic]\label{def:app-cg-power}
For a triple $(\ell_1, \ell_2, \ell)$ satisfying the triangle
inequality, write
$(\mathbf{F}_{\ell_1} \otimes \mathbf{F}_{\ell_2})\big|_\ell$
for the projection of the Kronecker product
$\mathbf{F}_{\ell_1} \otimes \mathbf{F}_{\ell_2}$ onto the
$D^\ell$-isotypic component, realised by contracting with the CG
coefficients $\CG{\ell_1}{m_1}{\ell_2}{m_2}{\ell}{m}$. The
\emph{CG power spectrum entry} is the squared norm of this projection,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:app-cg-power}
  P_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}
  := \lVert (\mathbf{F}_{\ell_1} \otimes \mathbf{F}_{\ell_2})|_\ell
     \rVert^2
  = \sum_{m=-\ell}^{\ell}
    \Bigl\lvert \sum_{\substack{m_1,m_2 \\ m_1+m_2=m}}
      \CG{\ell_1}{m_1}{\ell_2}{m_2}{\ell}{m}\;
      a_{\ell_1}^{m_1}\, a_{\ell_2}^{m_2} \Bigr\rvert^2.
\end{equation}
This is a degree-$4$, real-valued, $\SO(3)$-invariant polynomial in
the signal coefficients. The library appends a $O(L^2)$ family of CG
power scalars to the selective bispectrum unconditionally for
numerical robustness on the real-signal slice
(Remark~\ref{rem:app-Tell-rank-deficit}); they leave the asymptotic
output size unchanged.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Size of the full bispectrum]\label{prop:app-full-size}
The number of admissible triples $(\ell_1, \ell_2, \ell)$ with
$0 \le \ell_1, \ell_2, \ell \le L$ satisfying the triangle inequality
$|\ell_1 - \ell_2| \le \ell \le \ell_1 + \ell_2$ is $\Theta(L^3)$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
For fixed $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$, the triangle inequality admits
$\ell \in \{|\ell_1 - \ell_2|, \dotsc, \min(\ell_1 + \ell_2, L)\}$,
giving $\min(\ell_1 + \ell_2, L) - |\ell_1 - \ell_2| + 1$ valid
values of $\ell$. Summing:
\[
  N_{\mathrm{full}}
  = \sum_{\ell_1=0}^{L}\;\sum_{\ell_2=0}^{L}\;
    \bigl(\min(\ell_1+\ell_2,L) - |\ell_1-\ell_2| + 1\bigr)
  = \tfrac{1}{3}L^3 + \mathcal{O}(L^2).
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Invariance]
Each $\beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}$ is $\SO(3)$-invariant: the Wigner
matrices satisfy $D^{\ell_1} \otimes D^{\ell_2}
= \bigoplus_\ell D^\ell$ under the CG decomposition, so the triple
product contracted with CG coefficients is unchanged by
$\mathbf{F}_\ell \mapsto D^\ell(g)\mathbf{F}_\ell$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Why $\mathcal{O}(L)$ is impossible}
\label{sec:app-lower-bound}

\begin{proposition}[Dimensional lower bound]\label{prop:app-lower-bound}
Any \emph{smooth or polynomial} complete $\SO(3)$-invariant for
band-limited signals on $S^2$ at band-limit $L$ must have codomain
of dimension at least $(L+1)^2 - 3$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Let $\Phi : \R^{(L+1)^2} \to \R^k$ be a smooth (or polynomial)
$\SO(3)$-invariant map that separates orbits on a Zariski-open
dense subset $U\subset\R^{(L+1)^2}$. Smoothness implies $\Phi$ has a
well-defined generic rank $r := \max_{x\in U} \mathrm{rank}\,
d\Phi_x$; on the open dense subset where this rank is attained,
$\Phi$ is locally a submersion onto an $r$-dimensional submanifold,
and orbit-separation forces $r \ge \dim(\R^{(L+1)^2}/\SO(3))
= (L+1)^2 - \dim\SO(3) = (L+1)^2 - 3$
(generic-rank theorem for $\SO(3)$ acting on $V_L$;
cf.~Tarski--Seidenberg in the polynomial case). Hence
$k \ge r \ge (L+1)^2 - 3 = \Omega(L^2)$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The chain $\beta_{1,\ell,\ell+1}$ for $\ell = 0, \dotsc, L-1$ provides
only $\sim 3L$ scalar constraints (since each chain entry couples
$\mathbf{F}_1 \in \C^3$ with one known $\mathbf{F}_\ell$). This cannot
recover $(L+1)^2 \approx L^2$ unknowns.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Smoothness assumption]
The smoothness/polynomial qualifier is essential: pathological
non-measurable invariants (e.g.\ a fixed orbit-section indexed by
the axiom of choice) can in principle have codomain dimension $1$
but are not implementable. All practical bispectral or spectral
invariants are polynomial, so the proposition applies.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The selective index set}
\label{sec:app-selective-index}

The goal is to select, for each target degree $\ell\ge 8$, exactly
$2\ell{+}1$ bispectral triples $(\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell)$ such that the
selected entries are simultaneously linear in $\mathbf{F}_\ell$ (or in
$\conj{\mathbf{F}_\ell}$, which we conjugate) and have a coefficient
matrix depending only on already-recovered lower degrees. Combined
with a constant-size full-bispectrum seed at degrees
$\le L_{\mathrm{seed}}=\min(L,4)$, this gives an $O(L^2)$-sized
invariant matching the orbit-space lower bound of
Proposition~\ref{prop:app-lower-bound}.

\begin{definition}[Selective $\SO(3)$ bispectrum on
  $S^2$]\label{def:app-selective}
Set $L_{\mathrm{seed}} := \min(L,4)$. The \emph{seed index set} is
the full bispectrum truncated at $L_{\mathrm{seed}}$,
\[
  \mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{seed}}
  := \{(\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell) :
    0\le \ell_1 \le \ell_2 \le L_{\mathrm{seed}},\;
    \abs{\ell_1{-}\ell_2} \le \ell
      \le \min(\ell_1{+}\ell_2, L_{\mathrm{seed}})\},
\]
of size at most $(L_{\mathrm{seed}}{+}1)^3 \le 125$, independent of
$L$ once $L\ge 4$. This matches the implementation seed
$L_0 = 4$ of Algorithm~\ref{alg:so3-augmented}, on which
Bandeira et al.'s exact-arithmetic Gr\"obner-basis evidence for the
scalar bispectrum at low degrees~\citep{bandeira2017estimation} is
strongest. For each intermediate target $5\le\ell\le 7$ we use the
small explicit linear block
$\mathcal{T}_\ell^{\mathrm{small}}$ tabulated in
Algorithm~\ref{alg:so3-augmented}; for each high-degree target
$\ell\ge 8$ the \emph{bootstrap block}
$\mathcal{T}_\ell$ (also used in
Proposition~\ref{prop:app-entry-counts}) is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:app-Tell}
\begin{aligned}
  \mathcal{T}_\ell
  := &\ \{(a,\ell,\ell-a) : 1\le a \le \ell{-}1\} \\
   &\cup \bigl\{(a,\ell,\ell-a+1) : 2\le a \le \ell{-}1,
        \; a \ne (\ell+1)/2 \bigr\} \\
   &\cup \{(a,\ell-a,\ell) : 1\le a \le 4\} \;\cup\; \mathcal{Z}_\ell,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{Z}_\ell = \emptyset$ for $\ell$ even and
$\mathcal{Z}_\ell = \{(2,\ell{-}1,\ell)\}$ for $\ell$ odd ($\ell\ge 9$).
The exclusion $a\ne (\ell+1)/2$ for odd $\ell$ removes the
self-conjugate triple $(r,2r-1,r)$ at $\ell = 2r-1$, which has a
repeated index and is identically zero on real signals
(Proposition~\ref{prop:app-odd-vanishing}); the compensating row
$(2,\ell{-}1,\ell)\in\mathcal{Z}_\ell$ is admissible
($|\ell{-}1-2|\le\ell\le \ell{-}1+2$), has all-distinct indices, and is
not contained in the chain family above (which only covers
$(a,\ell{-}a,\ell)$ with $a=1,\dots,4$, i.e.\ pairs $(a,\ell{-}a)$ with
$a\le 4$). The size
counts to $(\ell{-}1)+(\ell{-}2)+4 = 2\ell{+}1$ for even $\ell$ and
$(\ell{-}1)+(\ell{-}3)+4+1 = 2\ell{+}1$ for odd $\ell\ge 9$. The bispectral
index set is
\[
  \mathcal{S}_\beta
  := \mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{seed}} \;\cup\;
     \bigcup_{\ell=5}^{L} \mathcal{T}_\ell,
\]
where $\mathcal{T}_\ell = \mathcal{T}_\ell^{\mathrm{small}}$ for
$5\le\ell\le 7$ (Algorithm~\ref{alg:so3-augmented}) and is given by
eq.~\eqref{eq:app-Tell} for $\ell\ge 8$.
The \emph{selective bispectrum} $\beta_{\mathrm{sel}}:V_L\to\R^N$ is
the concatenation of all selected scalar entries
$\{\beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}\}_{(\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell)\in\mathcal{S}_\beta}$;
by Lemma~\ref{lem:app-parity}, each entry contributes one real
component on the real-signal slice (real for even parity, purely
imaginary for odd parity). The optional CG power augmentation
(Definition~\ref{def:app-cg-power}) may be appended at $O(L^2)$ cost
without changing the asymptotic budget; the implementation does this
unconditionally, see the library implementation note below.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Library implementation note.}
Algorithm~\ref{alg:so3-augmented} below specifies the
implementation-grade index construction used by the
\texttt{SO3onS2} module. It augments
Definition~\ref{def:app-selective} in two ways: (i) it includes
\emph{mandatory even self-couplings}
$\beta_{\ell,\ell,\ell'}$ ($\ell'$ even, $2\le\ell'\le\ell$) and the
power entries $\beta_{0,\ell,\ell}$ for numerical robustness on the
real-signal slice, and (ii) it greedily appends CG-power entries to
$\mathcal{S}_P$ to maximise the per-degree real Jacobian rank. The
augmented invariant is a strict superset of $\beta_{\mathrm{sel}}$;
this is the structural index set used by the implementation.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Augmented selective index set for the $\SO(3)$ bispectrum
  on $S^2$ at band-limit~$L$. Returns bispectral triples
  $\mathcal{S}_\beta$ and CG power triples $\mathcal{S}_P$.
  This extends the BFS-based selective index construction of
  \citet{mataigne2024selective} from finite groups to $\SO(3)$ on $S^2$,
  adding the CG power augmentation step that is specific to real
  signals.}
\label{alg:so3-augmented}
\small
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\STATE \textbf{Input:} Band-limit $L$
\STATE \textbf{Output:} Bispectral set $\mathcal{S}_\beta$, CG power
  set $\mathcal{S}_P$
\STATE $\mathcal{S}_\beta \leftarrow \{(0,0,0)\}$;
  $\mathcal{S}_P \leftarrow \emptyset$
\STATE \textbf{Seed block:} add to $\mathcal{S}_\beta$ all triples
  $(\ell_1, \ell_2, \ell)$ with $0 \le \ell_1 \le \ell_2 \le L_0 = 4$
  and $|\ell_1 - \ell_2| \le \ell \le \min(\ell_1 + \ell_2, L_0)$
  \COMMENT{constant-size full-bispectrum seed}
\FOR{$\ell = 1, \dotsc, L$}
  \STATE $\text{budget} \leftarrow 2\ell + 1$
  \IF{$\ell = 4$}
    \STATE $\text{budget} \leftarrow 10$
      \COMMENT{keep all chain+cross at $\ell=4$}
  \ENDIF
  \STATE $\text{candidates} \leftarrow [\,]$
  \IF{$4 \le \ell \le 7$}
    \STATE append the explicit low-degree linear block
      $\mathcal{T}^{\mathrm{small}}_\ell$ to candidates
  \ELSIF{$\ell \ge 8$}
    \FOR{$a = 1, \dotsc, \ell{-}1$}
      \STATE append $(a,\, \ell,\, \ell{-}a)$ to candidates
    \ENDFOR
    \FOR{$a = 2, \dotsc, \ell{-}1$}
      \IF{$\ell$ is odd \AND $a = (\ell{+}1)/2$}
        \STATE \textbf{skip}
        \COMMENT{$(r,2r{-}1,r)\equiv0$ on real signals,
          Prop.~\ref{prop:app-odd-vanishing}}
      \ENDIF
      \STATE append $(a,\, \ell,\, \ell{-}a{+}1)$ to candidates
    \ENDFOR
    \FOR{$a = 1, \dotsc, 4$}
      \STATE append $(a,\, \ell{-}a,\, \ell)$ to candidates
    \ENDFOR
    \IF{$\ell$ is odd}
      \STATE append $(2,\, \ell{-}1,\, \ell)$ to candidates
        \COMMENT{compensate excluded $(r,2r{-}1,r)$ row}
    \ENDIF
  \ELSE
    \STATE append low-degree chain candidates
    \IF{$\ell \ne 2$}
      \STATE append cross candidates
        \COMMENT{$\beta_{1,2,1}$ excluded}
    \ENDIF
  \ENDIF
  \STATE append $(0,\, \ell,\, \ell)$ to candidates
    \COMMENT{power entry}
  \IF{$\ell \notin \{1,\, 2\}$}
    \FOR{$\ell' = \ell{-}1, \dotsc, 0$}
      \STATE append $(\ell,\, \ell,\, \ell')$ to candidates
    \ENDFOR
  \ENDIF
  \IF{$\ell = 2$}
    \STATE append $(\ell,\, \ell,\, \ell)$ to candidates
      \COMMENT{$\beta_{2,2,2}$}
  \ENDIF
  \STATE Remove entries that vanish for real signals; deduplicate
    against $\mathcal{S}_\beta$ already added by the seed block
  \STATE $\mathcal{S}_\beta \leftarrow \mathcal{S}_\beta
    \;\cup\;$ first $\min(\text{budget},\,
    |\text{candidates}|)$ live entries
  \STATE \COMMENT{\textbf{Mandatory even self-couplings}}
  \FOR{$\ell' = 2, 4, \dotsc, \lfloor\ell/2\rfloor \cdot 2$
    with $\ell' \le \ell$ and $\ell \ge 3$}
    \STATE $\mathcal{S}_\beta \leftarrow \mathcal{S}_\beta
      \cup \{(\ell,\, \ell,\, \ell')\}$
  \ENDFOR
  \STATE \COMMENT{\textbf{CG power augmentation}}
  \STATE Greedily add $P_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell'}$ (with
    $\max(\ell_1,\ell_2) = \ell$, $|\ell_1{-}\ell_2| \le \ell'
    \le \ell_1{+}\ell_2$) to $\mathcal{S}_P$ until the
    Jacobian rank of $\Phi_{\mathrm{aug}}$ at degree~$\ell$
    matches the number of unknowns
\ENDFOR
\RETURN $(\mathcal{S}_\beta,\, \mathcal{S}_P)$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{proposition}[Bootstrap-block entry count]\label{prop:app-entry-counts}
For each target degree $\ell\ge 8$, the bootstrap block
$\mathcal{T}_\ell$ defined in~\eqref{eq:app-Tell} contains exactly
$2\ell{+}1$ admissible triples and they are pairwise distinct.
Each triple in $\mathcal{T}_\ell$ has $\ell$ in exactly one of the
three slots, with the other two slots filled by indices strictly
smaller than $\ell$, so each entry contributes one scalar equation
linear in either $a_\ell^{m_2}$ or $\conj{a_\ell^m}$ with coefficients
depending only on lower-degree~$\mathbf{F}_{\ell'}$ ($\ell'<\ell$).
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The three subfamilies in~\eqref{eq:app-Tell} have sizes
$\ell{-}1$, $\ell{-}2$, $4$, summing to $2\ell{+}1$.
Triangle-inequality and bound checks ($a\le\ell{-}1$, $\ell{-}a\ge 1$,
$\ell{-}a{+}1\le\ell$) show every triple is admissible. Pairwise
distinctness is immediate from the disjoint patterns of the position
of the index $\ell$.  Linearity in the $\ell$-slot: the first two
families place $\ell$ in the $\ell_2$-slot
of~\eqref{eq:app-bispectrum}, so $a_\ell^{m_2}$ enters once with
known $a_a^{m_1}$ and $\conj{a_{\ell-a}^m}$ (resp.\
$\conj{a_{\ell-a+1}^m}$); the third family places $\ell$ in the
$\ell$-slot, so $\conj{a_\ell^m}$ enters once with known
$a_a^{m_1}$ and $a_{\ell-a}^{m_2}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Output size]\label{prop:app-output-size}
For every $L\ge 4$,
\[
  |\beta_{\mathrm{sel}}|
  \;=\; |\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{seed}}| + \sum_{\ell=5}^{L}(2\ell{+}1)
  \;=\; \Theta(L^2),
\]
counting one real component per scalar entry (real for even parity,
purely imaginary for odd parity, by Lemma~\ref{lem:app-parity}). This
matches the orbit-space lower bound $(L{+}1)^2-3$ of
Proposition~\ref{prop:app-lower-bound} up to an additive constant
(no factor-of-two from complex storage). For $L=4$ the sum is empty
and $|\beta_{\mathrm{sel}}|=|\mathcal{S}_{\mathrm{seed}}| = O(1)$.
The count matches the $30$ (resp.~$49$) live entries reported by the
implementation at $L=4$ (resp.~$L=5$).
\end{proposition}



\subsection{Parity structure of the bispectrum}
\label{sec:app-parity}

The selective construction of Section~\ref{sec:so3-selective}
exploits two structural facts about the scalar bispectrum
$\beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}$: a parity rule that fixes whether each
entry is real or purely imaginary on real signals (Lemma~\ref{lem:app-parity})
and the resulting identical vanishing of certain odd-parity entries
with a repeated index (Proposition~\ref{prop:app-odd-vanishing}). The
latter dictates the explicit exclusion in $\mathcal{T}_\ell$ at odd
target degrees, and the former is the source of the rank deficit
that motivates the CG-power augmentation of
Algorithm~\ref{alg:so3-augmented} (Remark~\ref{rem:app-Tell-rank-deficit}).

\begin{lemma}[Parity of the bispectrum]\label{lem:app-parity}
For any signal (real or complex):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:app-parity}
  \beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}(T_R \cdot f)
  = (-1)^{\ell_1+\ell_2+\ell}\,\beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}(f).
\end{equation}
For \emph{real} signals, there is an independent constraint:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:app-reality-constraint}
  \conj{\beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}(f)}
  = (-1)^{\ell_1+\ell_2+\ell}\,\beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}(f).
\end{equation}
Together:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,label=(\alph*)]
\item If $\ell_1+\ell_2+\ell$ is even:
  $\beta$ is real-valued (by~\eqref{eq:app-reality-constraint})
  and $T_R$-invariant (by~\eqref{eq:app-parity}).
\item If $\ell_1+\ell_2+\ell$ is odd:
  $\beta$ is purely imaginary (by~\eqref{eq:app-reality-constraint})
  and $\beta(T_R \cdot f) = -\beta(f)$, so $\im\beta$ flips sign under $T_R$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Under $T_R$, each $a_\ell^m \mapsto (-1)^m a_\ell^{-m}$.
Substituting into $\beta = \sum_m (\sum_{m_1+m_2=m} C\, a^{m_1} a^{m_2})
\conj{a^m}$, the three sign factors $(-1)^{m_1+m_2+m} = (-1)^{2m} = 1$
cancel. Re-indexing $m \mapsto -m$ and applying the CG symmetry
$\CG{\ell_1}{-m_1}{\ell_2}{-m_2}{\ell}{-m}
  = (-1)^{\ell_1+\ell_2-\ell}\,\CG{\ell_1}{m_1}{\ell_2}{m_2}{\ell}{m}$
yields~\eqref{eq:app-parity} (with $(-1)^{\ell_1+\ell_2-\ell} = (-1)^{\ell_1+\ell_2+\ell}$).

For real signals, $\conj{a_\ell^m} = (-1)^m a_\ell^{-m}$.
Taking the complex conjugate of $\beta$ and re-indexing similarly
yields~\eqref{eq:app-reality-constraint}.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[CG power is $T_R$-invariant]\label{lem:app-P-invariant}
For all triples $(\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell)$:
$P_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}(T_R \cdot f) = P_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}(f)$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
Under $T_R$, the CG-projected component transforms as
$(\mathbf{F}_{\ell_1} \otimes \mathbf{F}_{\ell_2})|_\ell^M
  \mapsto (-1)^{\ell_1+\ell_2+\ell+M}\,
  (\mathbf{F}_{\ell_1} \otimes \mathbf{F}_{\ell_2})|_\ell^{-M}$.
Taking the squared norm and re-indexing the sum over $M$
leaves $P$ unchanged.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Vanishing of odd-parity entries with a repeated index]
\label{prop:app-odd-vanishing}
Let $\beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}$ be the scalar bispectrum entry of a
real signal $f$ (so $\ell_1+\ell_2+\ell$ odd). If the index multiset
$\{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell\}$ contains a repeated value, then
$\beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}(f)\equiv 0$ as a polynomial in the SH
coefficients of $f$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Define the symmetric scalar 3j contraction
\[
  T_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell_3}(f) :=
  \sum_{m_1,m_2,m_3}
  \begin{pmatrix} \ell_1 & \ell_2 & \ell_3 \\
                  m_1 & m_2 & m_3 \end{pmatrix}\,
  a_{\ell_1}^{m_1}\, a_{\ell_2}^{m_2}\, a_{\ell_3}^{m_3} .
\]
Converting the Wigner~3j into a Clebsch-Gordan coefficient via
$\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} j_1 & j_2 & j_3 \\
       m_1 & m_2 & m_3\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)
= \frac{(-1)^{j_1-j_2-m_3}}{\sqrt{2j_3+1}}\,
  \CG{j_1}{m_1}{j_2}{m_2}{j_3}{-m_3}$ and applying the reality identity
$a_{\ell_3}^{m_3} = (-1)^{m_3}\,\conj{a_{\ell_3}^{-m_3}}$ shows
\[
  \beta_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}(f)
  = (-1)^{\ell_1+\ell_2}\sqrt{2\ell+1}\,
    T_{\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell}(f),
\]
so $\beta$ and $T$ have the same vanishing locus. The 3j symbol is
invariant under even column permutations and picks up
$(-1)^{\ell_1+\ell_2+\ell_3}$ under odd ones. If two columns $i,j$
satisfy $\ell_i=\ell_j$, the inserted vectors $a_{\ell_i},a_{\ell_j}$
are literally the same coefficient vector of $f$, so swapping those
two columns leaves the contracted sum $T$ unchanged while multiplying
the 3j prefactor by $(-1)^{\ell_1+\ell_2+\ell_3}$. For odd parity this
forces $T=-T$, hence $T\equiv 0$ and $\beta\equiv 0$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Consequences for $\mathcal{T}_\ell$]
\label{rem:app-Tell-zero-rows}
Proposition~\ref{prop:app-odd-vanishing} applies in particular to the
family $(r,2r-1,r)$: it has parity $4r-1$ (odd) and a repeated index,
so $\beta_{r,2r-1,r}\equiv 0$ on real signals. Numerical evaluation at
the seed-42 real witness confirms this: for $r=2,\dots,7$ the entry is
zero to double-precision rounding ($|\beta|\lesssim 10^{-14}$). Any
formal definition of $\mathcal{T}_\ell$ must therefore exclude these
triples (see~\eqref{eq:app-Tell}); we substitute a chain row with
all-distinct indices in their place.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[First nonzero odd-parity entry]\label{cor:app-first-odd}
The first bispectral entry with odd parity and generically nonzero imaginary
part occurs at degree $\ell = 4$:
$\beta_{2,3,4}$, with
$\ell_1+\ell_2+\ell = 9$ (odd) and
$\ell_1, \ell_2, \ell$ all distinct.
At the deterministic witness (seed~42), $\im(\beta_{2,3,4}) \approx 3.26 \ne 0$,
and $\im(\beta_{2,3,4})(T_R \cdot f) \approx -3.26$.
Similarly, $\beta_{2,4,3}$ and $\beta_{3,4,2}$ are nonzero odd-parity entries
at degree~$4$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[No parity-breaking at $\ell \le 3$]\label{rem:app-no-parity-breaking-le3}
For degrees $\le 3$, every odd-parity triple $(\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell)$
has at least two indices equal (since $\max(\ell_1,\ell_2,\ell) \le 3$
and $\ell_1+\ell_2+\ell$ odd forces at least one pair to be equal in this range).
By Proposition~\ref{prop:app-odd-vanishing}, all odd-parity entries vanish.
Therefore, the $T_R$ ambiguity cannot be resolved using only degrees $\le 3$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Why the augmented block is needed]
\label{rem:app-Tell-rank-deficit}
The bootstrap block $\mathcal{T}_\ell$ alone is rank-deficient on the
real-signal slice: at the seed-42 real witness, the
$(2\ell{+}1)\times(2\ell{+}1)$ submatrix $A_\ell$ assembled from the
$\mathcal{T}_\ell$ rows has SVD rank
$\approx \ell{+}2$ rather than $2\ell{+}1$ for $\ell\in\{8,\dots,15\}$
(verified numerically; the deficit is generic over real witnesses).
The mandatory even self-couplings and CG-power scalars are quadratic
or cubic in $\mathbf{F}_\ell$ and supply the additional independent
real equations needed to fill out the per-degree real Jacobian; the
augmented per-degree block (linear $\mathcal{T}_\ell$ plus quadratic
self-couplings plus CG-power scalars) is what
Algorithm~\ref{alg:so3-augmented} assembles, and is what the
implementation actually solves at degrees $\ell\ge 5$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Model variants}

All equivariant models share a $C_8$-equivariant
DenseNet~\citep{huang2017densenet} backbone with block configuration
$(4, 4, 4)$, 24 initial channels, and compression factor 0.5,
following the architecture of \citet{veeling2018rotation} but
replacing the final invariant map. Six model variants are evaluated:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Standard}: vanilla CNN with random rotation/reflection
    augmentation and global average pooling. No group dimension,
    so ${\sim}8\times$ fewer parameters at matched growth rate.
  \item \textbf{NormReLU}: $C_8$-equivariant backbone with norm-based
    nonlinearity and max pooling over the group dimension.
  \item \textbf{Gated}: $C_8$-equivariant backbone with gated
    nonlinearity (doubles internal channels) and group max pool.
  \item \textbf{Fourier-ELU}: $C_8$-equivariant backbone with
    Fourier-space ELU nonlinearity and group max pool.
  \item \textbf{Bispectrum}: $C_8$-equivariant backbone with ReLU
    nonlinearity and selective $G$-bispectrum via
    \texttt{CnonCn}$(n{=}8)$ as the terminal invariant map.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Training details}

To enable a controlled Pareto comparison, we sweep the DenseNet
growth rate $k \in \{3, 4, 6, 8, 12\}$ for equivariant models (and
$k \in \{6, 12, 20, 30, 35\}$ for the standard CNN to cover a
comparable parameter range), yielding models from ${\sim}30$K to
${\sim}1.6$M parameters. All models are trained on \textbf{10\% of
training data} (26{,}214 images) with AdamW~\citep{loshchilov2019adamw}
(lr $= 10^{-3}$, weight decay $10^{-4}$), cosine warm restarts,
mixed-precision training, and early stopping on validation AUC
(patience 10, max 50 epochs).

\subsection{Pareto analysis interpretation}

\emph{Architectural stability} (single seed, growth-rate sweep).
The bispectrum's AUC varies by only ${\sim}1$pp across a $12\times$
range of model sizes (80K--920K parameters), while norm swings by
$>$7pp. For practitioners, bispectral pooling ``just works'' without
growth rate tuning. The multi-seed matched-parameter results
(Table~\ref{tab:pcam-results}) confirm this: bispectrum and Fourier-ELU
have the lowest cross-seed variance ($\pm 0.004$).

\emph{Matched parameters.}
At matched parameters (${\sim}100$K), all equivariant models outperform
the standard CNN (AUC $0.896 \pm 0.010$, 3 seeds). Fourier-ELU leads
slightly ($0.945 \pm 0.004$), followed by NormReLU ($0.942$), bispectrum
and gated (both $0.941$). All equivariant methods cluster within
${\sim}0.5$pp of each other.

\emph{Scaling behavior.}
As parameters increase from the smallest to the largest configuration,
standard, norm, and Fourier-ELU all degrade (by 1.8, 2.6, and 0.9
AUC points respectively), while the bispectrum improves (+0.9 points,
reaching 0.943 at 920K params). This suggests that the bispectrum's
complete invariant map scales more gracefully with capacity.

\emph{Data scaling} (3 seeds).
At 1\% data the bispectrum leads all methods ($0.912 \pm 0.025$),
outperforming NormReLU ($0.873$), Fourier-ELU ($0.876$), and gate
($0.884$). By 10\% all equivariant methods converge (${\sim}0.941$--$0.945$).
The bispectrum's data-efficiency advantage narrows as data increases,
consistent with the hypothesis that complete invariants substitute for
missing training examples in the low-data regime.

\subsection{Hardware}

All experiments were run on a single NVIDIA H100 GPU (80\,GB).
Training wall-clock times range from ${\sim}$5 minutes (OrganMNIST3D,

\subsection{Data preprocessing}

\paragraph{PCam.}
PatchCamelyon~\citep{veeling2018rotation} consists of 327{,}680 RGB
patches of size $96 \times 96$ pixels extracted from whole-slide
images of lymph node sections~\citep{bejnordi2017camelyon}. We use
the standard train/val/test split. Images are loaded from HDF5 files
and normalised to $[0, 1]$. The standard (non-equivariant) baseline
uses random rotation and reflection augmentation during training; all
equivariant models are trained without augmentation.

\paragraph{OrganMNIST3D.}
OrganMNIST3D~\citep{medmnistv2} contains 1{,}742 abdominal CT organ
volumes of size $28 \times 28 \times 28$ voxels with 11 organ classes
(train/val/test = 971/161/610). Volumes are normalised to $[0, 1]$.
The standard baseline uses random octahedral augmentation (one of 24
rotations per sample); equivariant models are trained without
augmentation. Kernel rotations are implemented as exact voxel
permutations on the $3^3$ grid, avoiding interpolation artifacts.

\paragraph{Spherical MNIST.}
MNIST digits are projected onto the sphere via stereographic
projection following \citet{cohen2018spherical} and sampled on a
$64 \times 128$ equiangular grid. The selective bispectrum is
computed at $\ell_{\max} = 15$, producing 384 complex invariant
coefficients. The model input is obtained by applying
$\operatorname{sign}(x)\log(1+|x|)$ separately to the real and
imaginary parts of each coefficient (a sign-preserving, per-channel
injective transform), giving 768 real features. These features are
fed to a 3-layer MLP: Linear(768, 256)--BN--ReLU--Linear(256,
128)--BN--ReLU--Linear(128, 10). The power spectrum MLP uses the
same structure with hidden=128: Linear(16, 128)--BN--ReLU--Linear(128,
64)--BN--ReLU--Linear(64, 10). The standard CNN has 4 convolutional
layers (16--32--64--64 channels) followed by adaptive average pooling
and a Linear(1024, 128)--ReLU--Linear(128, 10) classifier.

\subsection{Aggregation procedures}

\paragraph{PCam.}
The Pareto figure (Figure~\ref{fig:pcam-pareto}) uses a single seed
(42) across the full growth-rate sweep. The matched-parameter table
(Table~\ref{tab:pcam-results}) and data efficiency analysis use 3 seeds
(42, 123, 456) per configuration.

\paragraph{OrganMNIST3D.}
All results in Table~\ref{tab:organ3d-results} report mean $\pm$ std
over 3 seeds (42, 123, 456). Rotation robustness
($\sigma_{\mathrm{rot}}$) is the standard deviation of test accuracy
across all 24 octahedral rotations of the test set. The data
efficiency analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:data-efficiency} in the main text) uses 3 seeds per data
fraction.

\paragraph{Spherical MNIST.}
All results in Table~\ref{tab:smnist-results} report mean $\pm$ std
over 3 seeds (42, 123, 456). Rotation robustness
($\sigma_{\mathrm{rot}}$) is measured by applying 10 random $\SO(3)$
rotations to the entire test set and computing the standard deviation
of accuracy, yielding 30 measurements per model (10 rotations
$\times$ 3 seeds).
